Module 01

What is a SOC?

A Security Operations Centre (SOC) is the nerve center of an organization's cybersecurity. It's a dedicated team — and often a physical room — where security analysts monitor, detect, investigate, and respond to cyber threats 24/7.

Simple Definition
Think of a SOC like a hospital emergency room — but for cyber incidents. Just like an ER has doctors monitoring patients and responding to emergencies around the clock, a SOC has analysts monitoring an organization's networks and systems, responding to security incidents before they cause serious damage.
Core Mission
DETECT threats before they cause damage

INVESTIGATE alerts to confirm real attacks

RESPOND to contain and remediate incidents

REPORT findings to management and stakeholders
Why Does Every Organization Need a SOC?
Threat RealityWhat It Means
A cyberattack happens every 39 secondsOrganizations are constantly targeted
Average breach detection time: 207 daysAttackers go undetected for months without a SOC
Average breach cost: $4.45 millionSOC investment pays for itself many times over
95% of breaches involve human errorSOC analysts catch what automated tools miss
SOC Operating Models
IN-HOUSE SOC
Organization runs its own SOC with internal staff. Full control, higher cost. Common in banks and govt.
MSSP / CO-MANAGED
Managed Security Service Provider. Outsourced SOC. Cost-effective. Good for SMBs.
VIRTUAL SOC
Remote team, no physical room. Flexible, modern model used post-pandemic.
💡 Interview Tip
When asked "What is a SOC?" — always mention the three pillars: People (analysts), Process (incident response procedures), and Technology (SIEM, EDR, firewalls). This shows structured thinking.
Module 02

SOC Fundamentals

Who works in a SOC, what do they do, and what tools do they use? Understanding the roles and technology stack is essential before your first day on the job.

// SOC Analyst Tiers
  • L1
    Tier 1 — Alert Triage Analyst (You!) First line of defense. Monitors alerts from the SIEM 24/7. Classifies alerts as True Positive or False Positive. Escalates real incidents to L2. Does NOT do deep investigation — focus is speed and triage. Skills: basic networking, log reading, following playbooks.
  • L2
    Tier 2 — Incident Responder Receives escalations from L1. Does deeper investigation. Hunts for attacker persistence. Performs containment. Writes detailed incident reports. Skills: forensics, threat hunting, advanced log analysis.
  • L3
    Tier 3 — Threat Hunter / Senior Analyst Proactively hunts for threats that evaded automated detection. Deep malware analysis. Creates new detection rules. Works with threat intelligence. Skills: reverse engineering, YARA rules, custom tooling.
  • MGR
    SOC Manager Manages the team, handles escalations, reports to CISO, manages budget and tooling. Ensures SLAs are met. Drives process improvement.
// Core Tools Stack

The brain of the SOC. Collects logs from ALL systems (firewalls, endpoints, servers, applications), correlates events, and generates alerts.

Module 06

Cyber Kill Chain

The Cyber Kill Chain (by Lockheed Martin) breaks every cyberattack into 7 stages. As a SOC analyst, you need to identify which stage an attack is in — and stop it as early as possible. Earlier = less damage.

💡 The Goal
Defenders win by "breaking the chain" at any stage. The earlier you break it, the better. Stage 1 break = attacker failed before doing anything. Stage 6 break = attacker already has access, much harder to remediate.
1
RECONNAISSANCE
Attacker gathers information about the target BEFORE attacking. Passive (Google, LinkedIn, WHOIS) or active (port scanning).
→ Example: Attacker Googles "ACME Corp employees" on LinkedIn, finds Sarah the IT admin. Notes her email format: s.jones@acmecorp.com
↓
2
WEAPONIZATION
Attacker creates the malicious payload — a weaponized document, phishing email, or exploit kit. No contact with target yet.
→ Example: Attacker crafts a fake "Salary Review.xlsx" with a malicious macro that downloads malware when opened.
↓
3
DELIVERY
The weapon is delivered to the target. Most common: phishing email. Others: USB drops, watering hole attacks, drive-by downloads.
→ Example: Attacker emails Sarah: "Please review the updated salary sheet from HR." The Excel file is attached.
↓
4
EXPLOITATION
The malicious payload triggers. Could be a macro running, a vulnerability being exploited, or simply a user clicking a link.
→ Example: Sarah opens the Excel file and enables macros. The malicious VBA script executes.
↓
5
INSTALLATION
Attacker installs malware (backdoor, RAT, rootkit) to maintain persistence — so they survive reboots and stay in the network.
→ Example: The macro downloads a RAT from pastebin.com and adds it to the startup registry. Now it runs every time Sarah logs in.
↓
6
COMMAND & CONTROL (C2)
The installed malware calls home to the attacker's C2 server. Attacker now has remote control of the victim machine.
→ Example: The RAT connects to 185.x.x.x over HTTPS every 5 minutes. Attacker's terminal shows Sarah's machine is online.
↓
7
ACTIONS ON OBJECTIVES
The attacker achieves their goal — data theft, ransomware deployment, lateral movement to more valuable targets.
→ Example: Attacker moves from Sarah's machine to the domain controller. Deploys ransomware across the entire org. Demands $500K.

Module 08

Incident Response Phases

Incident Response (IR) is the structured approach for handling a cyberattack. NIST defines 4 phases. SANS uses 6. Know both — interviewers will ask.

NIST Framework (4 Phases)
Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment, Eradication & Recovery → Post-Incident Activity
SANS PICERL (6 Phases)
Preparation → Identification → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned
🛠️
PHASE 1 — PREPARATION
Before any incident happens. Build your defenses, processes, and team readiness. This is the most important phase.
Actions: Write IR playbooks · Train analysts · Deploy SIEM/EDR · Create asset inventory · Define escalation paths · Run tabletop exercises
🔍
PHASE 2 — IDENTIFICATION / DETECTION & ANALYSIS
Detecting that an incident has occurred and determining its scope and severity. Is this a real incident or a false positive?
Actions: Monitor SIEM alerts · Triage alerts (True/False Positive) · Gather IOCs · Classify severity (P1/P2/P3) · Notify stakeholders · Document everything
🔒
PHASE 3 — CONTAINMENT
Stopping the spread. Short-term containment is fast (isolate machine). Long-term containment is more thorough (patch the vulnerability).
Actions: Isolate infected systems from network · Block attacker IPs/domains · Disable compromised accounts · Capture forensic image before cleanup · Short-term: stop the bleeding · Long-term: fix the root cause
🧹
PHASE 4 — ERADICATION
Completely removing the threat from all affected systems. Ensure no persistence mechanisms remain.
Actions: Delete malware files · Remove attacker accounts · Close exploited vulnerabilities · Patch all affected systems · Confirm no backdoors remain
🔄
PHASE 5 — RECOVERY
Restoring systems to normal operation. Verify they are clean before reconnecting to production.
Actions: Restore from clean backups · Rebuild affected systems if needed · Monitor closely post-recovery for recurrence · Gradually restore services · Confirm business operations normal
📋
PHASE 6 — LESSONS LEARNED (Post-Incident)
Review what happened, why it happened, and how to prevent it. Write a post-incident report within 2 weeks.
Actions: Timeline reconstruction · Root cause analysis · What detection/prevention failed? · Update playbooks · Recommend new controls · Debrief the team

Phishing Email Analysis

Phishing analysis is one of the most common L1 tasks. Users forward suspicious emails and you investigate. Here's a real annotated example and the exact process to follow.

FROM:no-reply@micros0ft-support.com ⚠ FAKE DOMAIN
TO:sarah.jones@acmecorp.com
DATE:Mon, 3 Mar 2025 02:14:37 +0000 ⚠ 2AM SEND TIME
SPF:FAIL ⚠ SPF FAILED
DKIM:FAIL ⚠ NOT SIGNED
DMARC:FAIL
⚠ URGENT: Your Microsoft 365 account will be suspended in 24 hours ⚠ URGENCY TACTIC
Dear Sarah, ⚠ WRONG NAME (generic)

We have detected suspicious activity on your Microsoft 365 account. Your account will be permanently suspended unless you verify your credentials within 24 hours. ⚠ FEAR + URGENCY

Click the link below to verify your account:

https://microsoft-account-verify.ru/login ⚠ .RU DOMAIN — NOT MICROSOFT

Microsoft Security Team ⚠ NO CONTACT INFO
// Phishing Investigation Process (L1 Analyst)
  • 01
    Check Email HeadersVerify SPF, DKIM, DMARC status. Check the actual "Reply-To" domain vs display name. Look at originating IP and mail server path. Tools: MXToolbox, Google Admin Toolbox.
  • 02
    Analyze the Sender DomainRun the sending domain through VirusTotal and WHOIS. How old is the domain? Attacker domains are often created hours/days before the campaign. Look for typosquatting (micros0ft, paypa1, g00gle).
  • 03
    Investigate URLs (Safely!)NEVER click directly. Use URLScan.io, VirusTotal URL scanner, or AnyRun sandbox. Check what the page looks like. Is it a credential harvester (fake login page)? Screenshot it as evidence.
  • 04
    Analyze Attachments (Safely!)Upload attachment hash to VirusTotal. Use Any.run or Hybrid Analysis for dynamic sandbox analysis. Check for Office macros (enable_macros = red flag), embedded links, or scripts.
  • 05
    Check if Users ClickedSearch SIEM/proxy logs for the malicious URL. Search email gateway logs to see who received the same email. Check EDR for any post-click process activity on affected machines.
  • 06
    Respond & DocumentIf phishing confirmed: Pull the email from all mailboxes (admin portal). Block sender domain and malicious URLs in email gateway + proxy. Reset credentials for users who clicked. Create and close the ticket with full IOCs documented.

Malware Analysis Fundamentals

As an L1 analyst, you won't do deep malware reverse engineering — but you need to know enough to triage a suspected malware alert, understand what it's doing, and escalate correctly.

Static Analysis
Analyzing the malware file WITHOUT running it. Safe because the malware doesn't execute.

What you check: File hash (MD5/SHA256), file type/magic bytes, strings inside the file (URLs, IPs, registry keys), PE header for Windows executables, signatures via VirusTotal.

VirusTotalPEStudiostrings command
Dynamic Analysis
Running the malware in a controlled sandbox and observing its behavior. Must be done in an isolated VM.

What you observe: Network connections made, files created/modified, registry changes, processes spawned, mutexes created.

Any.runCuckoo SandboxHybrid Analysis
Code Analysis
Reverse engineering the actual malware code. L3 level skill — you don't need this as L1.

Used by: Malware researchers, threat hunting teams, CTI analysts.

Ghidra (NSA)IDA Prox64dbg
L1 Malware Triage — Step by Step
  • 01
    Get the file hashSHA256 or MD5 hash of the suspicious file. Never share or upload actual malware files to the internet — just the hash.
  • 02
    Search VirusTotalIs the hash already known-malicious? If 40+ AV engines flag it = confirmed malware. Note the family name (e.g., Emotet, Cobalt Strike, AsyncRAT).
  • 03
    Check EDR process treeWhat process created the file? What did it spawn? Example: Word.exe → cmd.exe → powershell.exe → suspicious outbound connection = macro malware.
  • 04
    Check network connectionsWhat IPs/domains did the malware connect to? Search SIEM for those IPs across all endpoints — is this spreading?
  • 05
    Isolate and escalateIsolate the endpoint from the network via EDR. Document all IOCs (hashes, IPs, domains, filenames). Escalate to L2 with full context.

Reading and interpreting network logs is a daily SOC skill. You need to spot anomalies, identify attack patterns, and build a timeline of events from raw log data.

Firewall Log — Example & Analysis
# Timestamp SrcIP DstIP Port Action Bytes
2025-03-01 03:14:01 192.168.1.55 185.220.101.45 443 ALLOW 45.2KB
2025-03-01 03:14:12 192.168.1.55 185.220.101.45 443 ALLOW 52.1KB
2025-03-01 03:14:15 192.168.1.55 185.220.101.45 443 ALLOW 2.4GB ← !!EXFILTRATION!!
2025-03-01 03:15:00 10.0.0.1 192.168.1.55 22 ALLOW 1.2KB
2025-03-01 03:15:05 10.0.0.1 192.168.1.55 22 ALLOW 0.8KB
Red flags in this log:
1. 2.4GB upload at 3am to an external IP — clear data exfiltration indicator
2. 185.220.101.45 is a known Tor exit node IP (check on ThreatFox)
3. SSH connections FROM an internal server TO a workstation is backwards and suspicious
DNS Log — Detecting C2 Beaconing
# DNS Query Log
03:00:00 192.168.1.55 → a3f92k1.xyz → NXDOMAIN
03:05:00 192.168.1.55 → a3f92k1.xyz → NXDOMAIN
03:10:00 192.168.1.55 → a3f92k1.xyz → 185.x.x.x
03:15:00 192.168.1.55 → a3f92k1.xyz → 185.x.x.x
03:20:00 192.168.1.55 → a3f92k1.xyz → 185.x.x.x
Beaconing pattern detected: Exact 5-minute intervals = malware checking in with C2. Normal users don't query the same random-looking domain every 5 minutes. The random-looking domain (a3f92k1.xyz) is a DGA (Domain Generation Algorithm) — typical of malware like Conficker or Dridex.

Module 13

All Cyber Attacks

Complete reference: every attack type a SOC L1 analyst must know. Each includes definition, example, detection, prevention, and remediation.

WEBSQL Injection (SQLi)CRITICAL+
Attacker injects malicious SQL code into input fields to manipulate the database — exposing, modifying, or deleting data. Example: ' OR '1'='1 in a login field bypasses authentication entirely.
🔍 DETECT
  • WAF alerts for SQL keywords in inputs
  • Unusual DB errors in app logs
  • Large unexpected data responses
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Use parameterized queries / prepared statements
  • Deploy WAF
  • Least-privilege DB accounts
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Block offending IP via WAF
  • Patch vulnerable code
  • Audit DB for unauthorized access
WEBCross-Site Scripting (XSS)HIGH+
Attacker injects malicious JavaScript into a web page that runs in other users' browsers — stealing session cookies, redirecting victims, or performing actions on their behalf. Types: Stored, Reflected, DOM-based.
🔍 DETECT
  • WAF rules for <script> tags in input
  • CSP violation reports
  • Outbound requests to unknown hosts from browser
🛡️ PREVENT
  • HTML encode all output
  • Implement Content Security Policy (CSP)
  • HttpOnly & Secure cookie flags
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Remove malicious script from DB
  • Invalidate all active sessions
  • Notify affected users
WEBCSRF — Cross-Site Request ForgeryHIGH+
Tricks an authenticated user's browser into sending forged requests to a trusted site. Example: An image tag in an email triggers a bank transfer using the victim's active session cookie.
🔍 DETECT
  • Requests missing CSRF tokens
  • State-changing requests from unexpected referrers
  • WAF checking Origin/Referer headers
🛡️ PREVENT
  • CSRF tokens on all forms
  • SameSite=Strict cookie attribute
  • Validate Origin header
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Invalidate user session
  • Reverse unauthorized actions
  • Deploy CSRF protection in code
WEBCredential Stuffing / Brute ForceCRITICAL+
Credential stuffing uses leaked username/password lists to try logins on other sites (exploiting password reuse). Brute force tries all combinations. Example: LinkedIn breach credentials used to access Netflix accounts.
🔍 DETECT
  • High volume failed logins from single IP
  • Multiple accounts from same IP
  • Impossible travel alerts
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Enforce MFA on all accounts
  • Account lockout after N failures
  • CAPTCHA challenges
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Lock out affected accounts
  • Force password reset
  • Block attacker IPs via WAF
WEBDirectory TraversalHIGH+
Attacker uses ../../ sequences in URL parameters to access files outside the web root — like /etc/passwd or config files. Example: ?file=../../etc/passwd
🔍 DETECT
  • WAF rules for ../ patterns in URLs
  • Access logs showing /etc/ or C:\Windows\
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Validate/sanitize all file path inputs
  • Use allowlists for valid file names
  • Chroot web server process
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Block attacker IP
  • Audit what files were accessed
  • Patch vulnerable endpoint
WEBDDoS — Distributed Denial of ServiceHIGH+
Thousands of compromised machines (botnet) flood a server with traffic until it crashes. Example: 2016 Mirai botnet took down Dyn DNS, making Twitter/Netflix/Reddit unreachable for hours.
🔍 DETECT
  • Sudden traffic spike (Gbps alerts)
  • High CPU/memory on web servers
  • Flood from single IP range
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Cloudflare / AWS Shield DDoS protection
  • Rate limiting on all endpoints
  • CDN to absorb traffic
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Activate DDoS mitigation service
  • Block offending IPs via ACLs
  • Contact ISP for upstream scrubbing
CLOUDMisconfigured S3 Bucket / Cloud StorageCRITICAL+
Cloud storage set to public access lets anyone read/write data. Example: Capital One 2019 — misconfigured AWS WAF allowed SSRF → S3 data theft of 100M+ records.
🔍 DETECT
  • CloudTrail logs for unusual GetObject calls
  • AWS Config flagging public buckets
  • GuardDuty S3 anomaly alerts
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Enable Block Public Access by default
  • IAM least-privilege policies
  • CSPM tools for posture checks
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Set bucket to private immediately
  • Rotate all exposed credentials
  • Review CloudTrail for access history
CLOUDIAM Privilege EscalationCRITICAL+
Attacker exploits misconfigured IAM policies to grant themselves admin access. Example: User with iam:AttachUserPolicy permission attaches AdministratorAccess to their own account.
🔍 DETECT
  • CloudTrail: AttachUserPolicy, AddUserToGroup events
  • IAM changes outside change windows
  • AWS IAM Access Analyzer alerts
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Strict least-privilege IAM policies
  • Service Control Policies (SCPs)
  • MFA for all admin users
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Revoke escalated permissions immediately
  • Disable compromised IAM user
  • Rotate all access keys
CLOUDCloud Account HijackingCRITICAL+
Attacker gains access via stolen/exposed credentials or API keys. A dev pushes AWS keys to GitHub — bots detect within minutes and spin up 100 EC2 crypto-mining instances.
🔍 DETECT
  • Logins from unusual geolocations
  • Sudden billing spike
  • API calls at unusual hours
🛡️ PREVENT
  • MFA on all cloud accounts
  • Secret scanning on repos (GitGuardian)
  • AWS Secrets Manager — never in code
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Invalidate compromised credentials immediately
  • Terminate unauthorized instances
  • Review all actions in hijack window
CLOUDSSRF — Cloud Metadata AttackHIGH+
Server-Side Request Forgery tricks the server into querying the cloud metadata endpoint (169.254.169.254) — exposing IAM credentials. Used in the Capital One breach.
🔍 DETECT
  • WAF alerts for 169.254.169.254 in requests
  • Outbound requests from web servers to internal IPs
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Enable IMDSv2 (token-based metadata)
  • Block metadata IP at network level
  • Validate all user-supplied URLs
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Rotate exposed IAM credentials
  • Patch vulnerable application endpoint
NETWORKMan-in-the-Middle (MitM)CRITICAL+
Attacker secretly intercepts and relays communication between two parties. Both parties think they're communicating directly. Example: ARP spoofing on a corporate WiFi to intercept HTTP traffic between employees and the intranet.
🔍 DETECT
  • ARP table anomalies (duplicate MACs)
  • Unexpected SSL certificate changes
  • Network IDS alerting on ARP poisoning
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Use HTTPS everywhere (HSTS)
  • Dynamic ARP Inspection on switches
  • VPN for all sensitive communications
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Clear ARP cache on affected hosts
  • Identify and isolate rogue device
  • Force re-authentication for all users
NETWORKDNS TunnelingHIGH+
Attacker encodes data inside DNS queries to exfiltrate data or communicate with C2, bypassing firewalls that allow DNS. DNS port 53 is almost always open. Example: Malware encodes stolen data in subdomains: abc123data.evil.com
🔍 DETECT
  • Unusually long DNS query names
  • High volume of DNS queries to one domain
  • High entropy in subdomain strings
🛡️ PREVENT
  • DNS filtering/inspection tools
  • Block DNS-over-HTTPS to unknown resolvers
  • Monitor DNS query lengths
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Block malicious domain in DNS firewall
  • Isolate infected host
  • Investigate what data was exfiltrated
NETWORKPort Scanning / ReconnaissanceMEDIUM+
Attacker probes a target's open ports to map its attack surface before launching an actual attack. Tools: Nmap, Masscan. Usually the first step in an attack campaign.
🔍 DETECT
  • IDS alert: sequential port probes from one IP
  • Firewall logs: many blocked connections from one source
  • Network baseline anomaly alerts
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Close all unnecessary ports
  • Use port knocking for sensitive services
  • Geo-block or rate-limit external scans
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Block scanning IP in firewall
  • Review and close any unexpectedly open ports
  • Add IP to threat intel watchlist
NETWORKLateral Movement (Pass-the-Hash / PsExec)CRITICAL+
After gaining initial access, attacker moves to other systems in the network. Pass-the-Hash uses stolen NTLM hashes (without knowing the actual password) to authenticate to other Windows machines.
🔍 DETECT
  • Unusual SMB/RDP connections between internal hosts
  • PsExec or WMI execution via SIEM rule
  • Event ID 4625 failures across multiple hosts rapidly
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Network segmentation (VLANs)
  • Privileged Access Workstations (PAWs)
  • Disable NTLM where possible, use Kerberos
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Isolate all affected hosts
  • Reset all credentials that may have been used
  • Investigate full scope of lateral movement
MALWARERansomwareCRITICAL+
Encrypts victim's files and demands cryptocurrency ransom. Modern ransomware uses "double extortion" — steals data BEFORE encrypting it. Example: WannaCry 2017 hit 200,000 systems across 150 countries using EternalBlue SMB exploit.
🔍 DETECT
  • Mass file renaming / extension changes
  • vssadmin delete shadows command (EDR alert)
  • Spike in disk write activity from one process
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Offline immutable backups (3-2-1 rule)
  • Patch management — disable SMBv1
  • EDR with behavioral detection
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Isolate infected systems immediately
  • DO NOT pay ransom
  • Restore from clean pre-infection backups
MALWARETrojan HorseHIGH+
Disguises itself as legitimate software but contains hidden malicious code. Example: "Free VPN" app installs a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) in the background, giving attackers full control.
🔍 DETECT
  • Unexpected outbound connections to unknown IPs
  • New startup entries or scheduled tasks
  • EDR behavioral alerts
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Only install from official sources
  • Application allowlisting
  • Endpoint AV with real-time protection
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Quarantine infected machine
  • Remove persistence mechanisms
  • Reset all credentials used on machine
MALWARERootkitCRITICAL+
Hides itself deep in the OS (often kernel level), making it invisible to standard AV. Example: Sony BMG 2005 secretly installed a rootkit via music CDs. Once installed, safest fix is full OS reinstall.
🔍 DETECT
  • Rootkit-specific scanners (GMER)
  • Process list discrepancies (memory vs OS)
  • File integrity monitoring (Tripwire)
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Enable Secure Boot / UEFI
  • Keep OS and firmware updated
  • EDR solutions
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Complete OS reinstall recommended
  • Restore from known-good image
  • Never trust the infected system
MALWAREKeyloggerHIGH+
Records every keystroke silently — capturing passwords, credit cards, messages. Example: Employee opens malicious email → keylogger installs → within 24 hours attacker has banking credentials and VPN login.
🔍 DETECT
  • EDR detecting keyboard hook APIs (SetWindowsHookEx)
  • Unusual outbound connections from system
  • AV detection of known keylogger signatures
🛡️ PREVENT
  • MFA (captured password alone won't work)
  • Use virtual keyboards for sensitive inputs
  • Restrict software installation rights
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Change ALL passwords from a clean device
  • Enable MFA on all accounts
  • Remove keylogger via AV scan
MALWAREWormCRITICAL+
Self-replicating malware that spreads automatically across networks without user interaction. WannaCry used EternalBlue to spread from one machine to ALL machines on the same network — zero clicks required.
🔍 DETECT
  • Spike in internal network scanning traffic
  • Multiple machines infected in quick succession
  • IDS alerting on EternalBlue or SMB scanning
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Patch management (especially critical CVEs)
  • Disable SMBv1 and unnecessary protocols
  • Network segmentation
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Isolate infected network segment
  • Patch ALL systems before reconnecting
  • Identify patient zero and entry vector
PHISHINGGeneric PhishingHIGH+
Mass emails impersonating trusted entities (Microsoft, banks, IT) to trick users into revealing credentials or clicking malicious links. Low effort, high volume.
🔍 DETECT
  • Email gateway: SPF/DKIM/DMARC failures
  • Multiple users reporting same email
  • Proxy logs: clicks to known malicious URLs
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Deploy DMARC, SPF, DKIM
  • Email security gateway (Proofpoint)
  • Phishing awareness training
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Pull email from all mailboxes via admin
  • Reset credentials for users who clicked
  • Block sender domain in email gateway
PHISHINGSpear PhishingCRITICAL+
Highly targeted phishing — attacker researches the victim via LinkedIn/OSINT and crafts a personalized, convincing email. Much harder to detect than generic phishing.
🔍 DETECT
  • Email sandbox detonation of attachments
  • User reports of "suspicious but realistic" emails
  • Behavioral analytics: unusual login after email open
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Limit public OSINT exposure on LinkedIn
  • External email banners ("This email is from outside org")
  • Verify requests via phone on known numbers
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Isolate targeted user's machine if attachment opened
  • Analyze full email headers and attacker infrastructure
PHISHINGWhaling / CEO Fraud / BECCRITICAL+
Targets executives (CEO, CFO). Business Email Compromise (BEC) impersonates a CEO to authorize wire transfers. Ubiquiti lost $46.7M to this in 2015. No malware needed — just social engineering.
🔍 DETECT
  • Emails impersonating exec names from external domains
  • DMARC failures on emails claiming to be your domain
  • Finance team reports of urgent wire requests
🛡️ PREVENT
  • Dual approval for all wire transfers
  • Verify financial requests via phone
  • Strict DMARC policy enforcement
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Contact bank immediately to recall wire
  • Report to FBI IC3 and local law enforcement
PHISHINGVishing (Voice) & Smishing (SMS)HIGH+
Vishing: Phone calls impersonating IT support, banks, or government to steal credentials. Smishing: SMS phishing — fake delivery or bank messages with malicious links. AI voice cloning makes vishing increasingly convincing.
🔍 DETECT
  • Unusual logins after suspicious calls reported
  • MDM alerts for mobile users clicking SMS links
  • Help desk logs of callers requesting sensitive access
🛡️ PREVENT
  • IT policy: never ask for passwords by phone
  • Caller identity verification procedures
  • Train employees to hang up and call official numbers
🔧 REMEDIATE
  • Immediately reset any shared credentials
  • Brief all staff on the active campaign

Tools & Certifications

The industry tools you'll use on the job, and the certifications that will get you hired. Start with these — they are the most recognized and relevant for SOC L1 roles.

// Essential Tools for SOC L1
Splunk
SIEM · Log Analysis
Industry-leading SIEM. Learn SPL (Splunk Processing Language) to search and correlate logs. Free education at Splunk Training Portal.
Microsoft Sentinel
SIEM · Cloud-native
Azure-based SIEM, rapidly growing in enterprise. Uses KQL (Kusto Query Language). Free in Azure student sandbox.
CrowdStrike Falcon
EDR
Premier EDR. L1 analysts use it to view process trees, isolate hosts, and check threat intelligence on indicators.
Wireshark
Packet Analysis · Free
The standard for packet capture analysis. Free and open-source. Must-know for any SOC analyst — essential for network incident investigation.
VirusTotal
Threat Intelligence · Free
Analyze files, URLs, IPs, and domains against 70+ security engines. Daily tool for every SOC analyst. Free at virustotal.com.
TheHive / Cortex
Case Management · SOAR · Free
Open-source incident case management. Excellent for practicing IR documentation. Pairs with Cortex for automated enrichment.
Nmap
Network Scanning · Free
Network mapper. SOC analysts need to understand port scanning because attackers use it — and you'll see it in logs. Also used by defenders for asset discovery.
Any.run / Hybrid Analysis
Malware Sandbox · Free tier
Submit suspicious files and URLs for safe sandbox analysis. See what the malware does, what it connects to, what files it creates.
Elastic SIEM (ELK Stack)
SIEM · Free / Open Source
Elasticsearch + Logstash + Kibana. Great to learn at home. TryHackMe and BTLO have free Elastic labs. Common in smaller SOCs.
// Certifications — Roadmap for SOC L1
CompTIA Security+
COMPTIA · ENTRY LEVEL · MOST RECOMMENDED
The #1 starting point for cybersecurity careers. Covers threats, attacks, cryptography, network security, and IR. Required for many government and enterprise SOC roles. Globally recognized. Exam: SY0-701.
CompTIA CySA+
COMPTIA · INTERMEDIATE · SOC FOCUSED
Specifically designed for SOC analysts. Covers threat detection, behavioral analytics, incident response, and threat intelligence. The natural next step after Security+. Exam: CS0-003.
Google Cybersecurity Certificate
GOOGLE / COURSERA · BEGINNER · AFFORDABLE
8-course program on Coursera. Covers networking, Linux, SQL, SIEM tools, and Python. Excellent hands-on labs. Very affordable. Great first step before Security+.
Blue Team Level 1 (BTL1)
SECURITY BLUE TEAM · SOC SPECIFIC
Highly practical SOC-focused certification. Labs cover phishing analysis, SIEM investigation, network analysis, OSINT, and digital forensics. Hands-on exam — you investigate a real simulated attack.
SC-200 — Microsoft Security Operations Analyst
MICROSOFT · AZURE SENTINEL FOCUSED
Validates ability to use Microsoft Sentinel, Defender for Endpoint, and Azure security tools. Highly relevant if targeting enterprise SOC roles using Microsoft stack.
Splunk Core Certified User
SPLUNK · TOOL SPECIFIC
Validates Splunk searching, dashboards, and alerting. Many enterprise SOCs run Splunk. Having this cert signals you can operate the SIEM from day one — very attractive to employers.
